\begin{abstract}
We build and compare recurrent encoder--decoder models for English--Italian
translation on the Europarl v7 corpus. Starting from a vanilla RNN baseline we
show that a fixed context vector is the dominant failure mode, and address it
progressively with context feeding, LSTM units, pre-trained embeddings and
finally attention. We report BLEU and METEOR for every variant, compare both
translation directions, contrast word- and character-level models, and analyse
how performance degrades with sentence length. % TODO: add one sentence with the headline result once Tasks 4-5 are in.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% Keep this SHORT - the writing suggestions explicitly warn against long
% background sections. Three or four sentences is enough.
Machine translation maps a sentence in a source language onto a sentence in a
target language. Unlike classification there is no single correct output, so
translation quality is measured by comparing system output against reference
translations. We study English--Italian translation on the Europarl parallel
corpus, focusing on how architectural and representational choices affect
model behaviour rather than on maximising a single score.

\section{Data and Exploratory Analysis}
\label{sec:data}

The most informative asymmetry is lexical rather than positional. Italian uses
slightly \emph{fewer} tokens than English on average (25.13 vs.\ 26.05) yet
requires 94\% more distinct word types (170,625 vs.\ 88,040). This reflects
Italian's richer inflectional morphology: a single verb lemma surfaces as many
conjugated forms, each a separate entry for a word-level model, whereas English
marks the same distinctions with separate function words that are already in the
vocabulary.

This asymmetry is not merely descriptive; it sets a ceiling on what a word-level
model can do. With the 10,000-type vocabulary used in Section~\ref{sec:nmt},
5.18\% of Italian tokens fall outside the vocabulary against only 1.92\% of
English ones. Every such token is emitted as \texttt{<unk>} and is an
unrecoverable error, so the target side is intrinsically harder to generate than
the source side. Two predictions follow, both tested later: translating
\emph{into} English should outperform translating into Italian
(Section~\ref{sec:direction}), and a character-level model, which has no
vocabulary cap at all, should close part of the gap (Section~\ref{sec:char}).

We note that these counts are sensitive to tokenisation. Splitting on whitespace
alone conflates \texttt{session} with \texttt{session.}\ and inflates the type
counts roughly threefold (to 275,537 and 426,025) while doubling the apparent
OOV rate; the figures above separate punctuation.

The length distributions themselves are close to identical
(Figure~\ref{fig:length}): both peak around 20 tokens with a long right tail, and
per-sentence lengths are strongly correlated across the two languages
($r = 0.946$). Length is therefore a clean axis along which to analyse errors,
which we use in Section~\ref{sec:length}. The long tail also motivates the length
cap applied in Section~\ref{sec:nmt}: a small number of very long sentences
(up to 668 tokens) would otherwise dominate padding and training cost.

Because the full corpus is too large to train on, we randomly sample 10\% of
the aligned pairs (190,912 pairs, seed 42). Sampling is performed over
\emph{indices} so that pair alignment is preserved, and the drawn indices are
stored so the sample is exactly reproducible.

\section{Preprocessing}
\label{sec:prep}

Preprocessing removes a sentence pair whenever \emph{either} side is unusable,
which preserves alignment. We lowercase, normalise repeated whitespace, strip
leading and trailing whitespace, drop empty pairs, and drop pairs containing
XML-like lines. Of 190,912 pairs, 802 (0.42\%) were removed, all of them empty;
this release of Europarl contains no XML-tag lines, so that filter was a
no-op safety check.

We deliberately \emph{retain} punctuation, numbers and stopwords, and apply
neither stemming nor lemmatisation. This is the opposite of the choice one
makes for text classification, and for a specific reason: in translation every
token must be reproduced in the output. Function words carry grammatical
information the decoder needs to generate, and stemming would destroy exactly
the inflectional endings that Italian generation depends on.

\section{Attention}
\label{sec:attn}

The models of Section~\ref{sec:nmt} compress the entire source sentence into a
single fixed vector. We replace this with \textbf{Bahdanau (additive)
attention}: at every decoding step $t$ the decoder scores all encoder states
$h_1\dots h_S$ against its current state $s_t$,
$e_{t,i} = v^\top\tanh(W_s s_t + W_h h_i)$, normalises the scores with a softmax
into weights $\alpha_{t,i}$, and consumes the weighted sum
$c_t=\sum_i \alpha_{t,i}h_i$. Scores at padded positions are set to $-\infty$
before the softmax, so padding is never attended to. We chose the additive form
over a dot product because it does not constrain encoder and decoder hidden
sizes to match, and because its weights are sharper and therefore easier to
interpret.

To isolate the effect of attention we retrained the no-attention baseline
alongside it, with identical data splits, vocabularies, hyperparameters
(embedding 64, hidden 256, learning rate 0.001, batch 128, 12 epochs) and random
seeds. The only difference between the two systems is the attention mechanism.
Both are evaluated on the held-out test set. Training the baseline here rather
than reusing the Section~\ref{sec:nmt} model means both numbers come from the
same data, seed and hardware; as a check, our baseline reproduces the
Section~\ref{sec:nmt} result for the same architecture to within 0.3 BLEU,
which cross-validates the two implementations.

Attention improves BLEU from 4.16 to 7.68 ($+85\%$ relative) and METEOR from
18.76 to 27.07 (Table~\ref{tab:attention}). The cost is training time: because
the context now depends on the decoder state, the target sequence can no longer
be pushed through the LSTM in a single call and must be decoded step by step,
making an epoch roughly $2.6\times$ slower.

\subsection{Where the gain comes from}
\label{sec:attnlength}

If the fixed context vector really is the bottleneck, its cost should grow with
sentence length, and attention should therefore help long sentences most.
Figure~\ref{fig:bleulen} confirms this prediction. The absolute gain rises
monotonically across length buckets ($+2.9$, $+2.9$, $+3.6$, $+3.8$ BLEU), and
in relative terms the effect is much starker: $+13\%$ for sentences of 1--4
tokens against $+148\%$ for sentences of 15--20. Attention does not simply make
the model uniformly better; it specifically repairs the degradation that the
single-vector bottleneck causes on longer input.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{bleu_by_length.png}

advantage of attention grows with length, as the bottleneck explanation
predicts.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{What the model attends to}

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows the weights $\alpha_{t,i}$ for one test sentence.
A clear diagonal emerges, with individual weights reaching $0.9$: the model has
learned an approximately monotonic word alignment between English and Italian
even though it was never given alignment supervision. Alignment is a by-product
of learning to translate. The example also shows that alignment quality and
lexical quality are separable: the attention is well formed while the output
still contains a repetition loop (\emph{parlare di parlare di}), a common
failure of greedy decoding in an under-trained seq2seq model.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{attention_heatmap.png}
\caption{Attention weights for a test sentence. Rows are generated Italian
tokens, columns are source English tokens.}
\label{fig:heatmap}
\end{figure}

Two caveats. First, the attention model reaches its best validation loss at
epoch 8 and overfits thereafter, whereas the baseline's validation loss is still
decreasing at epoch 12; both systems are reported at their best checkpoint, but
the baseline had not fully converged under the shared epoch budget. The gap
between 4.16 and 7.68 BLEU is far too large to be attributed to this. Second,
all decoding is greedy; beam search would likely raise both systems and might
narrow the gap.
